The Bigger Picture. 

Adapting a general-purpose AI model to a specialized domain — here, the language of DNA and proteins — is usually assumed to come at a cost: teach it biology and it forgets how to reason about everything else. This “no free lunch” intuition shapes how practitioners budget compute and whether they attempt domain adaptation at all. We test the assumption directly, and without running any new training, by comparing three snapshots of the same model taken before and after biological training. The result overturns the intuition: feeding the model raw biological sequence made it better at general knowledge, at writing code, and even changed how it reasons — producing shorter, more decisive chains of thought without losing accuracy. The gains appear during the sequence-pretraining stage and are partly given back during task-specific fine-tuning, revealing that the two stages play complementary rather than interchangeable roles. This suggests a broader principle for data-centric AI: structured scientific data — biological sequence today, and by extension code, mathematics, and chemistry — is not merely knowledge to be absorbed but a lever that reshapes what a foundation model can do.

Abstract 18 

It is widely assumed that continued pretraining (CPT) on a narrow, out-of-distribution corpus 19 

such as raw biological sequence must trade away a general-purpose model’s broad competence — 20 

the “alignment tax” or catastrophic-forgetting intuition. We test this directly, without any new 21 

training, by re-analyzing three checkpoints from a single lineage of a 26B-parameter Mixture-of-22 

Experts model (Gemma-4-26B-A4B): the instruction-tuned base, the same model after biological 23 

CPT (8.7B tokens of DNA, protein, and biomedical text), and after subsequent supervised 24 

fine-tuning (SFT). Across three independent capability axes — general knowledge/reasoning 25 

(MMLU, ARC, HellaSwag), code generation (MBPP), and biomedical knowledge (BixBench) 26 

— we find that biological CPT does not degrade the model; it lifts it: MMLU +13 points, 27 

MBPP pass@1 nearly doubles ( 0.33 → 0.63 ), and BixBench discrimination rises sharply (MCC 28 

0.23 → 0.92 ). The single measured regression is truthfulness (TruthfulQA −8.8 points), a small 29 

and interpretable domain drift. A clean vocabulary-expansion ablation ( < 0.4 pt on every general 30 

metric) confirms the gains are attributable to CPT, not tokenizer changes. Crucially, subsequent 31 

SFT narrows the model back: all three axes fall to near-base levels, revealing a consistent division 32 

of labor — CPT re-organizes and lifts the shared capability substrate; SFT cashes 33 

it out onto target tasks . We argue this reframes biological sequence not as a competitor 34 

for a foundation model’s capacity but as a form of structured scientific data that reshapes its 35 

capability profile, and that CPT and SFT should be budgeted as complementary rather than 36 

substitutable stages. All checkpoints, evaluation code, and per-example outputs are public. 37 

Keywords: foundation models, continued pretraining, catastrophic forgetting, data-centric AI, 38 

mixture-of-experts, capability transfer, structured scientific data. 39 

# 1 Introduction 40 

The dominant intuition about continued pretraining (CPT) on a narrow domain corpus is subtractive: 41 

pouring out-of-distribution tokens — raw protein and DNA sequence, which share almost no surface 42 

statistics with natural language — into a general-purpose model is expected to erode its broad 43 

competence, a phenomenon variously described as the alignment tax or catastrophic forgetting [ 1, 2 ]. 44 

Under this view, domain adaptation is a trade: one buys biological ability at the cost of general 45 

ability, and the practitioner’s job is to minimize the damage. 46 

This paper argues that, for a modern Mixture-of-Experts foundation model, the intuition is 47 

wrong — or at least incomplete. We show, by re-analysis of existing checkpoints and without any 48 

new training , that biological CPT on a 26B-parameter model does not merely avoid catastrophic 49 

forgetting; it improves the model on capability axes that have nothing to do with biology, including 50 

general knowledge (MMLU [ 3]) and code generation (MBPP). We then show that the subsequent 51 

supervised fine-tuning (SFT) stage, far from adding capability, narrows the model back toward its 52 

base competence on held-out axes. The picture that emerges is a clean division of labor between the 53 

two training stages, and it suggests a reframing of what biological data is to a foundation model. 54 

Our substrate is a Mixture-of-Experts model [ 4 –6 ] (Gemma-4-26B-A4B [ 7 ]); the analysis is 55 

deliberately training-free, re-using an existing CPT/SFT lineage [ 2] so that every capability difference 56 

is attributable to a single stage. This complements router-level interpretability of the same model 57 

family [ 8– 10] by asking not where computation is organized but how the training data reshapes 58 

what the model can do. 59 

Related work. Our claim sits at the intersection of three literatures. Continued pretraining 60 

and forgetting : domain-adaptive CPT is standard practice [ 2 , 11, 12], but is widely reported to 61 

risk catastrophic forgetting of general ability [ 1, 13], motivating replay and balancing schemes. 62

# 4 Discussion 166 

4.1 A division of labor between CPT and SFT 167 

The recurring shape of our results — base < BioCPT > SFT on every held-out axis — points to 168 

a division of labor that is usually conflated. Continued pretraining does not simply add domain 169 

facts; it re-organizes and lifts a shared capability substrate that is then visible on tasks (general 170 

knowledge, code) far from the pretraining domain. Supervised fine-tuning does the opposite: it 171 

narrows the model onto the target task distribution, trading breadth on held-out axes for focus on 172 

the served task. Neither stage is a substitute for the other, and budgeting them as interchangeable 173 

— or omitting CPT to “save compute” — misreads their roles. This is consistent with, and sharpens, 174 

prior router-level evidence on this model family that CPT reshapes middle-layer computation while 175 

SFT re-aligns the output. 176

4.2 Biological sequence as structured scientific data 177 

If narrow, out-of-distribution biological sequence raises general and coding competence, then the 178 

“narrow domain = capacity competition” framing is inadequate. We propose instead that biological 179 

sequence functions as structured scientific data : a corpus whose value to a foundation model lies not 180 

(only) in the facts it carries but in the structural regularities it forces the model to internalize — 181 

regularities that transfer to other structured/formal languages such as code. Under this view biology 182 

is simply the first representative member of a broader class (code, mathematics, chemistry/SMILES), 183 

and the natural next question is not “does biology help biology” but “how does the composition of 184 

scientific-data CPT shape the capability profile of a foundation model.” 185 

4.3 Limitations 186 

This is a training-free re-analysis of one lineage of one 26B MoE model; the CPT / SFT recipe is fixed, 187 

so we characterize this lineage rather than establishing a scaling law. Part of the ARC/HellaSwag 188 

gain is a format effect (instruct-vs-completion loglikelihood), which we flag and which does not affect 189 

the knowledge-heavy MMLU gain. Zero-shot sequence-homology is not measurable by simple probes, 190 

so that axis is argued indirectly. TruthfulQA is a genuine, if small, regression and we do not hide 191 

it. Establishing the optimal CPT data mixture, and testing whether the CPT-lifts / SFT-narrows 192 

pattern generalizes across model sizes and domains, is the subject of the mixture-pretraining program 193 

we outline as future work. 194
